PSYCHOMETRIC PRINCIPLES FOR EXAM QUALITY

RELIABILITY:
Reliability refers to the consistency of an assessment. A reliable exam produces similar results
under consistent conditions.

Types of reliability:
- Internal consistency (Cronbach's Alpha): measures whether items in the exam correlate with each
  other. Alpha >= 0.70 is acceptable; >= 0.80 is good for high-stakes exams.
- Test-retest reliability: same students get similar scores on re-administration.
- Inter-rater reliability: different markers give similar scores (especially for essays/open-ended).

Improving reliability:
- Use more items (longer tests are generally more reliable).
- Use clear marking rubrics.
- Standardize exam conditions.
- Remove items with very low or negative discrimination indices.

VALIDITY:
Validity is the degree to which the exam measures what it claims to measure.

Types of validity:
- Content validity: does the exam cover the full range of learning outcomes?
- Construct validity: are the questions actually testing the intended cognitive skills?
- Criterion validity: do scores predict future performance or real-world competence?

Improving validity:
- Map each question explicitly to a learning outcome and Bloom's level.
- Involve subject experts in question review.
- Review pass rate and DI data to identify questions with validity concerns.

STANDARD ERROR OF MEASUREMENT (SEM):
SEM estimates the precision of individual scores. Lower SEM = more precise.
Formula: SEM = SD * sqrt(1 - reliability)
Use SEM to set grade boundaries with appropriate confidence intervals.

GRADE BOUNDARY SETTING:
- Use criterion-referenced (not norm-referenced) standards where possible.
- Set boundaries based on what a minimally competent student should achieve.
- Conduct standard-setting exercises (e.g., Angoff method) for high-stakes exams.
- Document and justify boundary decisions for accountability.
