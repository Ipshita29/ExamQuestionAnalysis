BLOOMS TAXONOMY AND ASSESSMENT DESIGN

Bloom's Taxonomy provides a hierarchical framework for categorizing educational objectives. It is widely
used to design assessments that measure different cognitive levels.

LEVELS (from lowest to highest order):
1. Remember — Recall facts and basic concepts (define, list, identify)
2. Understand — Explain ideas or concepts (describe, summarize, classify)
3. Apply — Use information in new situations (solve, demonstrate, use)
4. Analyze — Draw connections among ideas (differentiate, organize, compare)
5. Evaluate — Justify a decision or course of action (argue, defend, critique)
6. Create — Produce new or original work (design, construct, plan)

ASSESSMENT DESIGN PRINCIPLES:
- A well-balanced exam should cover multiple Bloom's levels. Over-reliance on Remember/Understand
  leads to surface learning and poor discrimination.
- Higher-order questions (Analyze, Evaluate, Create) tend to have lower pass rates but higher
  discrimination indices, which is desirable.
- Questions should map clearly to learning objectives stated in the course syllabus.
- Avoid ambiguous wording; it increases construct-irrelevant variance.

DIFFICULTY DISTRIBUTION BEST PRACTICE:
- Easy (pass rate > 0.85): should comprise no more than 15% of total marks.
- Hard (pass rate < 0.30): should comprise no more than 20% of total marks.
- Medium: the bulk (60-70%) of the assessment.
- Aim for an overall mean score between 55% and 70% for a balanced exam.

ITEM IMPROVEMENT GUIDELINES:
- Too Easy question: raise cognitive level, add constraints, increase specificity.
- Too Hard question: break into sub-questions, provide scaffolded hints, revise language clarity.
- Confusing/Poor Discrimination question: review distractors (for MCQ), check for double-barrelled
  wording, pilot with a focus group.
- Excellent question: preserve and reuse in future assessments as an anchor item.
