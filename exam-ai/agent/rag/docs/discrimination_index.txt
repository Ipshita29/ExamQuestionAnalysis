DISCRIMINATION INDEX — INTERPRETATION AND IMPROVEMENT

The Discrimination Index (DI) measures how well an exam question distinguishes between high-performing
and low-performing students.

CALCULATION:
DI = (Mean score of top 27% students - Mean score of bottom 27% students) / Max possible marks

INTERPRETATION:
- DI >= 0.40 : Excellent — strongly discriminates; keep as is.
- 0.30 <= DI < 0.40 : Good — reasonably discriminating.
- 0.15 <= DI < 0.30 : Marginal — needs review.
- DI < 0.15 : Poor — does not discriminate; revise or remove.
- DI <= 0 : Negative — bottom students outperform top students; item is flawed.

COMMON CAUSES OF LOW DISCRIMINATION:
1. Question too easy — all students answer correctly; no spread.
2. Question too hard — all students fail; no spread.
3. Ambiguous wording — students guess randomly.
4. Misaligned to taught content — tests content not covered in the course.
5. Double-barrelled items — tests multiple unrelated concepts simultaneously.

STRATEGIES TO IMPROVE DISCRIMINATION:
- Rewrite items to target higher Bloom's levels.
- For MCQs: improve distractors (plausible wrong answers based on common misconceptions).
- Split complex questions into component parts.
- Conduct item analysis post-exam and retire items with DI < 0.10 after two administrations.
- Use anchor items (DI >= 0.40) to calibrate exam difficulty across years.

RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN PASS RATE AND DISCRIMINATION:
- An ideal question: pass rate 0.40–0.75, DI >= 0.30.
- Very easy questions inherently have low DI (no spread to discriminate).
- Very hard questions inherently have low DI (all fail, no spread).
- Target the sweet spot of moderate difficulty for maximum utility.
